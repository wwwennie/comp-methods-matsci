\subsection{Context and Motivations}\label{context-and-motivations}}

In the last problem set, we considered the properties of perfect cubic
solids. We now consider the properties of non-perfect solids, e.g.,
solids with defects. We similarly need to find the minimum of energy
with respect to not only the lattice parameters but also with respect to
the atom positions in the vicinity of the defect.

Consider a solid with a vacancy. We can create a vacancy by simply
removing one of the atoms in our FCC supercell structure. All atoms
around that vacancy will relax away from the equilibrium positions of
the perfect solid. The change in energy between the system with the
vacancy and the system without the vacancy is then the vacancy defect
formation energy (excluding a chemical potential of the removed atom).

Suppose we want to find the vacancy formation energy in a system with
atoms that interact with the Lennard-Jones potential

\begin{equation}
\phi(r) = \frac{B}{r^{12}} - \frac{A}{r^6}
\end{equation}

where the input is $r$ and the output is $\phi$. Often, the
Lennard-Jones potential is written into a dimensionally more useful form
that depends on two parameters: $\sigma$, which is the distance at
which the potential is zero $\phi(\sigma) = 0$, and $\epsilon$,
which is the absolute value of the minimum of the potential with units
of energy. With these two parameters, the Lennard-Jones potential looks
like

\begin{equation}
\phi(r) = 4\varepsilon\Big[ \Big(\frac{\sigma}{r}\Big)^{12} - \Big(\frac{\sigma}{r}\Big)^{6}\Big],
\end{equation}

where $\sigma = (B/A)^{1/6}$ and $\epsilon = A^2/4B$. The
Lennard-Jones Potential is sometimes written in an equivalent form in
terms of the position of the minimum $r_m$. One may easily derive that
$r_m = 2^{1/6} \sigma$ and $\phi(r_m) = -\epsilon$ where
$\epsilon$ corresponds to the well depth,

\begin{equation}
\phi(r) = \epsilon\Big[ \Big(\frac{r_m}{r}\Big)^{12} -2 \Big(\frac{r_m}{r}\Big)^6\Big].
\end{equation}


We need to set up a system from which we can remove the atom and then
compare the energy of that system ($E_{vac}$) with the defect-free
sysm ($E_{bulk}$). In the case of a vacancy in the FCC cell, the
vacancy formation energy may be computed as

\begin{equation}
 \Delta U = E_{vac} - \frac{n-1}{n} E_{bulk} 
\end{equation}

where the chemical potential of the removed atom has been subsumed in
the second term.

We will use an FCC lattice with periodic boundary conditions. Because
the system is periodic, introducing the vacancy will also create a
vacancy in any periodic image leading to a vacancy concentration of
$X_V = 1/n$ where $n$ is the number of atoms in the simulation cell.
Clearly, the values of $X_V$ simulated are far larger than those found
in real systems. To test whether we are simulating the dilute limit
(i.e., isolated defects), we do a convergence test of the simulation
cell size until the energy converges. More advanced methods can also be
used, such as embedding the small system into an elastic continuum.

    \hypertarget{exercises-non-cubic-solids}{%
\subsection{Exercises: non-cubic
solids}\label{exercises-non-cubic-solids}}

    Suppose we want to model a system that is not a cubic structure. As
outlined in the textbook, we can write the atomic positions in fractions
of the unit cell as done before. The distance between two atoms with
indices $i$ and $j$ in the full, non-cubic cell is

\begin{equation}
r_{ij}^2 = \vec{s}_{ij}^T \mathbf{G} \vec{s}_{ij} = \vec{s}_{ij} \cdot (\mathbf{G} \vec{s}_{ij})
\end{equation}

where $\vec{s}_{ij} = \vec{s}_j - \vec{s}_i$ and $\mathbf{G}$ is the
metric tensor

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{G} =
\begin{pmatrix}
 a^2 & ab\cos(\gamma) & ac\cos(\beta) \\
ab\cos(\gamma) & b^2 & bc\cos(\alpha) \\
ac\cos(\beta) & bc\cos(\alpha) & c^2 
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

where $a,b,c,\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are the standard lattice constants.

We can evaluate the distance between two atoms $r_{ij}$ as follows.
Suppose we consider a vector $\vec{t} = (t_1,t_2,t_3)$ and a
$3\times 3$ matrix $\mathbf{G}$, we have

\begin{equation}
\vec{t}^T \mathbf{G} \vec{t} = \vec{t} \cdot (\mathbf{G} \vec{t}) = t_i \cdot G_{ij} t_j
\end{equation}

where repeated indices are summed. As $\mathbf{G}$ is a symmetric
matrix, $G_{ij} = G_{ji}$, we can explicitly evaluate the above terms.

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\vec{t}^{T} \mathbf{G} \vec{t} 
& = G_{11} t^2 + G_{22}t_2^2 + 2G_{12}t_1 t_2 + 2 G_{13} t_1 t_3 + 2G_{23} t_2 t_3 \\ 
& = a^2 t_1^2 + b^2 t_2^2 + c^2 t_3^2 + 2ab\cos(\gamma)t_1 t_2 + 2ac\cos(\beta)t_1 t_3 + 2bc\cos(\alpha)t_2 t_3 
\end{split}
\end{equation}

Thus, in order to evaluate the distances between atoms $r_{ij}$, we
replace
\texttt{dist = a * sqrt(xij**2 + yij**2 + zij**2);} with

\begin{verbatim}
dist = sqrt(a**2*xij**2 + b**2*yij**2 + c**2*zij**2
         + 2.*a*b*cos(gamma)*xij*yij
         + 2.*a*c*cos(beta)*xij*zij
         + 2.*b*c*cos(alpha)*yij*zij);
\end{verbatim}
